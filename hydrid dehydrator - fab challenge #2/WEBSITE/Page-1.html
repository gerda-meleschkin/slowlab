The Hotbox - A modular dehydrator & incubator

The Hotbox is a low-energy solution for food preservation, biomaterial creation and a controlled space for microorganisms. 

Concept

The purpose of the Hotbox is to provide an easy to assemble and reproduce alternative to energy costly dehydrators and incubators, utilising as much as possible the energy of the sun while monitoring the temperature and the humidity of the boxes to guarantee the efficiency of the devices.

What is it 
meant to do?

The Hotbox draws heat from the sun and concentrates it in order to dry foods, like fruits and vegetables, so they can be stored for long periods of time. Running on solar power makes this food dryer efficient and inexpensive. Additionally it uses the leftover heat of the dehydrator box to provide a stable, warm, and sufficiently humid environment for microorganisms in an incubator. The Hotbox also features a heater that will supplement the solar heat in case the temperature dips.

The master plan

We drew inspiration from the Open Source Beehive Project for the design. Like a beehive, it needs to stack neatly on top of each other and the bottom boxes are meant to have an effect on the top boxes. 

Planning

We broke down the plan of the project in several parts:

1. A cardboard prototype of the design where we could test the measurements, the wire placements, the fan, and the order of the boxes.
2. A circuit diagram of all the necessary electronical components and their wiring
3. The logic coding flowchart of the relation between the boxes. Defining what the necessary temperature and humidity levels need to be.

Execution

Electronics Process

We decided to put all the main controller electronics parts in an independent modular box of the “Hotbox”, under the dehydrator and the incubator, because we want to be able to exchange solar module parts when we use it outdoors. 

We address the function of the electronics in two parts.
1. The Warmth System is managed by a PID controller and the temperature pad.
       a. PID controller. (AC 220V)
       b. Heat pad. 12V - 5A
2. The Cooling System is managed by the microcontroller ESP32 with the temperature and humidity sensor DHT11, a relay, a fan, a power supply, and wires.
       a. ESP32 Arduino Board. 3.3 V
       b. DHT11 Digital Temperature Humidity Module Sensor. 5V
       c. Relay Module.
       d. Fan. DC12V - 0.28A
       e. AC-DC Power Supply 12V - 16.7A

Process
Day 1.
 - Agreeing as a group on the general idea. 
The initial idea on day 1 was: we are going to heat the base module and position a fan in that same module to push the hot air to the upper boxes.
 - Starting to collect the components we were going to need:
 heat bed, power supply and the electronic kit we had with the ESP32.
Point of attention: For the choice of the power supply, first check all the components you are going to use to see how much Amps they need, then choose the power supply accordingly.In our case we also needed a voltage converter as the power supply we had provided 12V (which was good to power the heat mat and the fans) but the ESP32 only needed 3,3V. As we didn’t have any, for a first version we decided to power them separately.

Execution

Day 2.
 - Changing strategy for the fans and heat bed position: 
as the incubator and the dryer need different temperature and humidity conditions, we decided to regulate the temperature in each box by using the fans to cool them down. The heat bed would still be positioned in the base module box and would be set up manually to the target temperature with a PID and there would be one temperature & humidity sensor and a fan in each box (dryer and incubator) to regulate.

 - Wiring the components and programming the microcontroller: As we didn’t have the sensor we needed on that day, we started the wiring using a photoresistor instead to make sure we could connect everything and that we could program the fans to be triggered for a certain value of the sensor. We used a relay between the power supply, the fan and the microcontroller to be able to control when the fan turns on or off .

Day 3.
 - Wiring the final the right sensors : We bought two temperature & humidity DHT11 sensors to be able to finalize the circuit.
 - Programming: We installed the library DFRobot_DHT11 to use the new sensors.
 - Creating the logic diagrams for both the dryer and the incubator modules (see diagram image) after managing to make the circuit work for a simple test condition.
- Programming the conditions in two different files for the dryer module and the incubator module.*
- Wiring and testing the PID controller with the heat bed and the power supply.
- Simplifying the wiring: Once everything was tested and working, we removed the breadboard, soldered the connections that could be assembled and joined positive & negative connections in a connector.
- Duplicatin g the same wiring for the second sensor & fan module .

Fabrication

Fabrication steps:

- Designing the  model
- Nesting all items with little spaces on the CAM software
- Define how deep every file should be milled
- Use rhino cam to set up the speed/feed/properties/order etc
- Create the g-code (1.screws to attach the board to the Maschine 2.the whole milling)
- Set the x,y,z axes in the Maschine
- Upload the 1. And the 2. G-code

Creativity

We inserted creativity in our project through the act of joining together equipments that are usually thought of as opposites (a dehydrator that needs low humidity vs an incubator that requires high  humidity) while trying to keep it as low energy as possible.

Considerations & Mistakes

Cut the pieces before sending the complete file to the CNC machine to test the tolerance, and avoid the possibility of sanding after. There was no time to do this due to the line for using the machine and the time it requires to cut.

Estimate better the time, and organize better the time.

The design process in Rhino7 was developed mostly for one person, it would be interesting to share in real time the process, in order to optimize the time of designing. 

More intermediate team check-ups would have helped reorganize the work and find solutions all together to finish the object! We did this the last day with the electronics team helping the modeling team to design the missing components.
